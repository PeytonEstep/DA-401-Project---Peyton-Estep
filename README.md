Background
	Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly used as search engines, decision aids, and now mental health support. A recent study found that 13.1% of US youths used generative AI for mental health advice, with 65% of those engaging monthly and 92.7% finding the advice useful (McBain et al., 2025). This raises concerns about whether AI can replicate the risk assessment capabilities of traditional clinical practice. Recent cases have intensified these concerns, including one where a 14-year-old boy died by suicide after months of interacting with an AI chatbot that encouraged his suicidal plans (Payne, 2024). Cases like this highlight the sycophantic nature of AI, where chatbots are overly agreeable and flattering toward users (Mello-Klein, 2026). While these behaviors might seem supportive, they can quickly become dangerous when users rely on chatbots for emotional validation. While there are a few AI-based apps that are designed to provide CBT interventions, like Woebot and Therabot, it’s important to understand that these systems are embedded with established therapeutic frameworks, unlike open-ended generative LLMs. In fact, generative AI has been found to reproduce and amplify stigma toward individuals with mental health disorders, with one study finding that LLMs were more likely to conceptualize Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD) as less treatable and associating Schizophrenia and Alcohol Use Disorder (AUD) with violence (Lauderdale et al., 2025; Moore et al., 2025). 

The Current Study
	This paper will examine whether AI sycophancy increases for stigmatized diagnoses compared to more common disorders like Depression and Anxiety. Through my research, I aim to fill the gap in AI safety by testing whether the “helpfulness” incentive of models overrides safety protocols when the user presents with high-complexity logic, like paranoid ideation or treatment resistance. This is important to research because a model that validates a user’s decision to discontinue medication or engage in social isolation poses a risk to the user’s safety. By utilizing a 1-3 Likert scale to score 3000 unique AI responses across 30 prompts, this project will contribute to our understanding if how AI safety filters can be improved to recognize and restructure cognitive distortions instead of echoing them.
